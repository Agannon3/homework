Andrew's Response

I always thought of comics to be the outcast of media forms.
Looking back on my childhood, It was not surprising to me that
I was heavily interested in
superhero comicbooks and BONE!
My dad would buy me some on fridays
at a famous NYC comicbook store,

(I dont know if its there anymore on 34th and 8th????....a victim of Amazon perhaps?).
One of the things McCloud points out that I like about comics is how we
use our eyes to interact with the text in a way that is specifically spatial.
Unlike a book, where dialogue and action is constrained by sequential elements of textual
writing such as sentences, paragraphs, and quotes. The comic uses images, drawings, and quotes
to bring traditional static elements of literature to life.

McCloud calls comics a "mono-sensory medium."
Despite using one of our senses there is very little that is monolothic
regarding how a comic may effect us.

He shows how words like "short" and "long" have first and fourth dimension implications.
The shapes and sizes used in this medium allow us to experience text
in far different ways then traditional novels or texts.
Writing using different fonts, sizes, and script adds a flavor to comics
that classical traditional novels do not have.
Yet there is an element of comics and comic readers that makes me
feel the genre is disrespected or even dismissed by society and academia.

I remember when my 4th grade teacher said "read real books not comics."
Imagine how differently The Great Gatsby would be percieved
if it was a comic.
Comics are an important part of our communcation history as McCloud
makes astute connections to Egyptian Hieroglyphs.
Respect Comics! Welcome the outcast!

Andrew Gannon September 14th, 2020.
